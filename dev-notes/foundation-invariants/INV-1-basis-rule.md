## INV-1. Basis rule — every load-bearing claim carries a named basis

**Invariant.** Every load-bearing claim and every design premise carries
a named basis — the evidence it rests on; an assertion without one stands
as an assumption, not a basis.

**Kernel home.** `spec/core.md` §3.2 ("The basis rule"); restated in
`spec/glossary.md`:53 ("The basis rule is specified in `core.md` §3.2.").

**External anchor.** Zave & Jackson, "Four Dark Corners of Requirements
Engineering," ACM TOSEM 6(1):1–30 (1997) — *designation*. Every primitive
term must be given a written, maintained, clear-and-precise informal
explanation grounding it in real-world phenomena; the paper notes most
requirements methods fail to enforce this. The assertion-vs-definition
line — a grounded assertion can be true or false and must be validated; an
ungrounded definition cannot be false but may be useless or misleading —
is anneal's grounded-and-checkable vs free-floating-assertion line.
